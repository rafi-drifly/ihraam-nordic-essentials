Umrah is performed in a clear, fixed order: enter the state of Ihram at or before the Miqat and make the niyyah (intention), perform Tawaf (seven circuits) around the Ka'bah, pray two rak'ah near Maqam Ibrahim, complete Sa'i (seven trips) between Safa and Marwah, and finally shave or trim your hair to exit Ihram. Follow that sequence and, in the mainstream view, your Umrah is complete.

This guide walks through each step in plain language for Muslims travelling from Europe, with practical comfort tips and honest notes on where the details deserve a scholar's word.

What are the steps of Umrah?

Umrah has four core acts that every pilgrim performs in the same order: entering Ihram with the intention for Umrah, Tawaf around the Ka'bah, Sa'i between Safa and Marwah, and Halq or Taqsir (shaving or trimming the hair). Between and around these, there are recommended prayers and du'a that make the journey richer. Knowing the sequence before you land removes most of the anxiety, so you can focus on worship rather than logistics.

Below, each step is explained on its own. Where the fiqh gets detailed, or your personal situation is unusual, ask your group guide or a knowledgeable scholar rather than guessing.

Step 1: Entering Ihram and making the niyyah

Ihram is both a physical state and an inner intention. Before you cross the Miqat, the appointed boundary around Makkah, you enter this state. Many pilgrims prepare at the airport or on the plane if their route crosses the Miqat in the air, so it helps to know your Miqat in advance and change into your garments early.

Preparation is simple: trim the nails, remove unwanted hair, and perform ghusl (a full wash) if you can, or at least wudu. Then you put on the Ihram clothing and make the niyyah for Umrah in your heart. Saying a short phrase of intention aloud, such as "Labbayk Allahumma Umrah," is a common practice. From this moment the restrictions of Ihram apply: no perfume, no cutting hair or nails, no arguing, and no marital relations, among others.

Once in Ihram, begin reciting the Talbiyah often. Men raise their voices with it, women keep it quieter. Keep it on your tongue on the way to the Haram.

Step 2: Entering Masjid al-Haram and first sight of the Ka'bah

Enter the mosque with your right foot first, as is recommended when entering any masjid, and with a calm, grateful heart. When your eyes first fall on the Ka'bah, it is a deeply moving moment. Many pilgrims pause to make heartfelt du'a here, since this is a time when supplication is hoped to be answered. There is no single obligatory phrase you must recite, so raise the du'a that is closest to your heart, and keep it sincere.

Stop the Talbiyah as you are about to begin Tawaf. Then move calmly toward the starting point without rushing or pushing others.

Step 3: Tawaf, seven circuits around the Ka'bah

Tawaf is walking seven times around the Ka'bah anticlockwise, keeping it on your left. Each circuit starts and ends at the corner of the Black Stone (Hajar al-Aswad).

  • Begin each circuit level with the Black Stone. If you can reach it, touch or kiss it; if not (which is usual in crowds), simply point toward it with your right hand and say "Allahu akbar." Never harm yourself or others to reach the stone.
  • Idtiba (for men) means uncovering the right shoulder by passing the rida under the right arm, worn throughout Tawaf.
  • Raml (for men) is a brisk, proud walk with short quick steps in the first three circuits, then normal walking for the remaining four. Where the crowd is too dense to move freely, do what you safely can.

There is no fixed script of du'a for Tawaf, so make du'a and dhikr freely in any language. Between the Yemeni corner and the Black Stone, many recite the well-known Qur'anic supplication asking for good in this world and the next. Women perform Tawaf the same way but without idtiba and raml.

Step 4: Two rak'ah at Maqam Ibrahim, then Zamzam

After completing the seventh circuit, pray two short rak'ah, ideally behind Maqam Ibrahim if space allows, or anywhere in the mosque if it is crowded. Then go to drink Zamzam water. Drinking your fill and making du'a while you drink is a cherished practice. Pace yourself here; the mosque is large and the day is long.

Step 5: Sa'i between Safa and Marwah

Sa'i is walking seven trips between the two small hills of Safa and Marwah, recalling Hajar's search for water for her son Ismail. You start at Safa and finish at Marwah. Safa to Marwah counts as one trip, Marwah back to Safa is the second, and so on until you reach Marwah on the seventh.

  • At Safa, face the Ka'bah, raise your hands, and make du'a. There is no obligatory formula, so ask for what you need.
  • Men jog lightly between the two green markers along the way; women walk at a steady pace throughout.
  • The path is air-conditioned and smooth, but it is long, so save some energy for it.

Make du'a and dhikr freely as you go. When you reach Marwah on the seventh trip, your Sa'i is done.

Step 6: Halq or Taqsir to exit Ihram

The final act is cutting the hair. Halq means shaving the head, and Taqsir means trimming it. Men may do either, and shaving carries a greater reward, though many trim. Women do not shave: they gather their hair and trim about a fingertip length (roughly 2 to 3 cm) from the ends. Once you have done this, you leave the state of Ihram and its restrictions lift. Your Umrah is complete.

Tip: Bring a small comfort kit: a refillable water bottle, comfortable sandals you can slip on and off easily, and a mind set on patience with the crowds. Hydrate steadily, especially in the heat, and keep your group's meeting point clear in case you get separated.

Frequently asked questions

How long does Umrah take to perform?

The rites themselves often take two to four hours once you begin Tawaf, depending heavily on the crowds. During peak seasons like Ramadan it can take much longer. Arrive rested, allow extra time, and do not rush the worship.

What do I wear for Ihram?

Men wear two unstitched white cloths: the izar for the lower body and the rida for the upper body, with nothing tailored underneath. Women wear ordinary modest clothing that covers the body, with the face and hands uncovered during the rites in the majority view. Soft, breathable fabric makes the long day easier.

Where do I make the intention for Umrah?

You make the niyyah as you enter the state of Ihram, at or before crossing the Miqat. If your flight crosses the Miqat in the air, prepare beforehand so you are not caught unready. Knowing your specific Miqat before travelling avoids confusion.

Do women perform Umrah differently?

The core steps are the same for women and men. The differences are that women wear regular modest dress rather than the two-cloth Ihram, they do not do idtiba or raml in Tawaf or jog in Sa'i, they recite the Talbiyah quietly, and at the end they trim a fingertip length of hair rather than shaving. A woman on her menses delays Tawaf until she is pure, so ask a scholar about timing.

What if I make a mistake during Umrah?

Minor slips are common and often easily corrected, and some may require an expiation, so do not panic. The safest course is to ask your group guide or a knowledgeable scholar on the spot, since the right response depends on which step was affected and how. Intending well and seeking guidance is always the sound approach.

Conclusion

Umrah is a short but profound journey, and its steps are easier to hold in mind once you see them in order: Ihram and intention, Tawaf, the two rak'ah and Zamzam, Sa'i, then Halq or Taqsir. Prepare your heart, prepare your body, and lean on your group guide or a trusted scholar whenever a detail is unclear. May Allah accept your Umrah and make it a means of nearness to Him.
