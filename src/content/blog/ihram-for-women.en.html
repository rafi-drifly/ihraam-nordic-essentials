For women, ihram is refreshingly simple in one sense: there is no special uniform to buy. But that simplicity raises real questions. What counts as acceptable? Can you wear your normal abaya? Do you cover your face? What about menstruation?

This guide answers each one plainly, sticking to the mainstream position and pointing you to a scholar where personal circumstances matter.

Is there a specific "ihram" dress for women, or just modest clothing?

There is no prescribed ihram garment for women. Unlike men, who must wear two specific unstitched white cloths, a woman enters ihram in ordinary modest clothing. The Prophet ﷺ said, in the well-known narration, that a woman's ihram is in her face and hands, meaning her restrictions relate mainly to covering the face and hands, not to a special outfit.

What your clothing must do is meet the normal conditions of modest dress:

  • Loose enough not to show the shape of the body
  • Thick enough not to be see-through
  • Covering the whole body except, in the standard view, the face and hands
  • Clean and, once in ihram, free of perfume or scent

So a comfortable abaya or jilbab with a headscarf is completely valid. Many women simply wear what they would already wear for prayer, chosen for comfort in the heat.

What colours are allowed — does it have to be white or black?

Any modest colour is allowed. There is no Shariah requirement for women to wear white or black; the idea that women must wear a particular colour is custom, not a ruling.

That said, practical choices tend to win out. Many women choose black, beige, white, grey, or other neutral tones because they are simple, do not draw attention in crowds, and hide dust and sweat well over long days. Bright or eye-catching prints are best avoided, not because a colour is forbidden but because the spirit of ihram is humility and not standing out.

Can I wear a regular abaya and hijab?

Yes. A regular abaya and hijab are the most common ihram clothing for women and are entirely acceptable, as long as they meet the modesty conditions above. A few practical tips:

  • Pick breathable fabric. Makkah is hot and you will be walking and standing for long stretches. Lightweight, natural or moisture-wicking fabrics are far more comfortable than heavy synthetics.
  • Wide sleeves help. Loose sleeves let you draw your hands into them when needed without wearing gloves.
  • Bring a spare. A second abaya means you are covered if one gets soiled or wet.
  • Secure your hijab simply. Avoid heavily pinned or decorative styles that are hard to adjust during tawaf and sa'i.

Do I need to cover my face? What about gloves?

Face: In the state of ihram, a woman does not wear a fitted niqab or burqa that is shaped to the face. The face is normally left uncovered during ihram. However, the widely accepted practice is that if non-mahram men pass close by, a woman may drape a loose cloth or the edge of her khimar down over her face and then lift it again afterwards. The distinction is between a fitted, tied face-veil (avoided in ihram) and a loose covering let down when needed (permitted by the majority).

Gloves: Gloves are not worn in the state of ihram; the hands stay uncovered. If you wish to cover your hands when men are near, use the long sleeves of your abaya rather than fitted gloves. Because families and groups sometimes follow different scholarly opinions on the details, confirm the exact practice with your group's scholar if you are unsure.

What footwear is appropriate for women?

Women have more flexibility than men here. There is no requirement to leave the top of the foot exposed, so ordinary closed, comfortable footwear is allowed. The priority is comfort and safety on hard marble that becomes very hot in the sun:

  • Choose cushioned, well-fitting shoes or sandals you have already worn in.
  • Closed footwear is fine and can protect your feet from the heat of the ground.
  • Keep them plain and modest rather than decorative.

How do I handle menstruation-related clothing concerns?

Menstruation does not prevent you from entering ihram. A menstruating woman still makes the intention, enters the state of ihram, and travels with everyone else. What changes is that she delays tawaf (which requires purity) until her period ends, while she can perform other parts such as the standing at Arafat during Hajj.

  • Wear reliable protection. Sanitary products are entirely permitted in ihram; managing your period cleanly is expected and encouraged.
  • Pack extra changes. Bring more than one abaya and spare underclothes so you can stay fresh over long days away from your room.
  • Dark neutral outerwear helps for peace of mind.
  • Anything to delay your period is a personal medical decision to make with a qualified professional, and its permissibility is something to raise with a scholar.

Menstruation is a natural state and no barrier to a complete, rewarded pilgrimage. If you are unsure how it affects a specific rite on a specific day, ask your group's scholar.

Frequently asked questions

Is there a specific ihram dress for women?

No. A woman has no prescribed ihram garment. She enters ihram in ordinary modest clothing that is loose, opaque, and covers the whole body except, in the standard view, the face and hands.

Does women's ihram have to be white or black?

No. There is no Shariah colour requirement for women. Any modest colour is allowed; many choose black, beige, white or grey simply for practicality.

Do women have to cover the face during ihram?

A woman does not wear a fitted niqab in ihram and normally leaves the face uncovered. She may drape a loose cloth over her face when non-mahram men pass close, then lift it again.

Can women wear gloves in ihram?

No. Gloves are not worn in ihram; the hands stay uncovered. Use the long sleeves of your abaya to cover the hands when needed.

Can a menstruating woman enter ihram?

Yes. She makes the intention and enters ihram like everyone else, but delays tawaf, which requires purity, until her period ends. Sanitary products are permitted in ihram.

Conclusion

For women, ihram is about modest, comfortable, unscented clothing worn with humility, not a special uniform. Choose breathable fabric, pack a spare, keep the face and hands uncovered (draping loosely when needed), and don't let menstruation worry you: it is no barrier to a full and rewarded pilgrimage.
