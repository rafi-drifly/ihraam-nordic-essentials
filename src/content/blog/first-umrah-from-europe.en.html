What is Umrah, in brief?

Umrah is the lesser pilgrimage to Makkah. Unlike Hajj, which happens on fixed days of the year, Umrah can be performed at almost any time. It is a beloved act of worship, and while it is not obligatory in the way Hajj is for those who are able, many scholars regard it as highly recommended. The core rites are few and clear: entering the state of Ihram at the appointed boundary (the Miqat), performing Tawaf (circling the Ka'bah seven times), performing Sa'i (walking between Safa and Marwah seven times), and then Halq or Taqsir (shaving or trimming the hair) to exit Ihram.

The whole sequence can be completed in a few hours, though most pilgrims stay several days to pray at the Sacred Mosque, drink Zamzam, and often continue on to Madinah. Learning the rites in advance matters more than anything you can buy, so give yourself unhurried time at home to read and understand each step.

How do I get there? Visas and the Nusuk platform

For most European travellers, the two common routes are a dedicated Umrah visa or a tourist eVisa, both of which now permit Umrah. The official gateway is the Saudi government's Nusuk platform (and the linked Nusuk Umrah services), where you create an account, submit your details, and book approved packages or the visa itself.

Visa rules, fees, and eligibility change from season to season, so treat any figure or requirement you read online (including here) as a starting point only. Always confirm the current rules on official Saudi sources or the Nusuk platform before you pay for anything.

Tip: Start your visa application well before your intended travel dates. Passports usually need several months of validity remaining, and processing times vary, so early is always safer than last minute.

Booking: independent trip or group package?

You have two broad choices, and both are valid.

  • Group package. A reputable Umrah agency handles your visa, flights, hotels near the Haram, transfers, and often a guide who walks you through the rites. For a first-timer this removes a great deal of stress and is what many families choose.
  • Independent booking. You arrange the eVisa yourself through Nusuk, book your own flights and hotel, and organise transfers. This gives you flexibility and can cost less, but it asks more of you in planning and confidence.

Flights from Europe are straightforward. Direct and one-stop routes run from hubs such as Stockholm, Copenhagen, Oslo, Helsinki, London, Frankfurt, Amsterdam, and Istanbul into Jeddah (the closest airport to Makkah) or Madinah. If you fly into Jeddah, you will typically pass a Miqat on the way, so know in advance whether you will enter Ihram at the airport, on the plane, or at your hotel beforehand.

Budgeting at a high level

Costs vary widely with season, hotel distance from the Haram, and how far in advance you book, so rather than quote fixed prices, plan around these categories:

  • Visa and any associated insurance or service fees.
  • Flights, which swing most with timing: peak periods like Ramadan and school holidays are the most expensive.
  • Accommodation, where being closer to the mosque costs more but saves walking.
  • Local costs: transfers, food, a local SIM or eSIM, and gifts.
  • Ihram and essentials, which you can prepare cheaply and calmly from home before you fly.

Booking outside peak season and a little further from the Haram is the simplest way to bring the total down without affecting your worship.

When should I go?

Umrah is available almost year-round, and the best window depends on your priorities: cooler weather, smaller crowds, lower prices, or the immense reward of Ramadan. Each season has trade-offs, and there is no single right answer. For a fuller look at the seasons, weather, and crowd patterns, see our dedicated timing guide linked at the end of this article.

What should I pack, and how do I prepare my Ihram?

Pack light and deliberately. Beyond your normal travel documents and modest clothing, a first-time pilgrim's essentials include:

  • Your Ihram garments (for men, two seamless white cloths; for women, ordinary modest clothing serves as Ihram, with the face and hands kept as the mainstream position advises).
  • An unscented soap, since fragrance is avoided once in Ihram.
  • Comfortable, easy-off sandals or slippers for the mosque.
  • A small bag for shoes, a refillable bottle for Zamzam, and any regular medication.

Prepare and wash your Ihram before you fly. New cloth can carry sizing or residue, so wash it once at home with an unscented detergent, let it dry fully, and pack it clean. What to expect on arrival and during the rites

After landing and clearing immigration, you will travel to Makkah, enter Ihram if you have not already, and make your intention (niyyah) for Umrah, reciting the Talbiyah. At the Sacred Mosque you perform Tawaf around the Ka'bah, pray near the Maqam Ibrahim area, drink Zamzam, then perform Sa'i between Safa and Marwah, and finally trim or shave your hair to complete the Umrah.

Expect crowds, long walks, and moments of overwhelming emotion. Move at a steady pace, keep hydrated, and do not rush. If you booked a group, your guide will lead each step; if you are independent, a signposted, well-practised crowd around you makes it easier than it sounds. Health, vaccinations, and staying connected

Saudi Arabia sets health requirements for pilgrims that can change year to year, sometimes including specific vaccinations. Check the current official requirements and speak to your doctor or a travel clinic in good time before you go. Build in basic self-care too: comfortable footwear, sun protection, and steady hydration prevent most first-timer discomfort.

To stay connected, a Saudi eSIM or local SIM is inexpensive and keeps you reachable for your family and group. Download an offline map and any du'a or rites app before you travel, so you are not dependent on signal inside the crowds.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need a special Umrah visa, or can I use a tourist visa?

Both routes are commonly used by European pilgrims. A dedicated Umrah visa and the tourist eVisa both currently allow you to perform Umrah, and both are handled through the official Nusuk platform. Because rules change, confirm the current options on official Saudi sources before applying.

How long does a first Umrah trip usually take?

The rites themselves can be completed in a few hours, but most first-time pilgrims plan a stay of several days to a week. This allows time to rest after the flight, pray at the Sacred Mosque, and often visit Madinah. Your visa validity and flight dates will shape the exact length.

Should I book a group package or travel independently?

For a first Umrah, a reputable group package removes most of the logistical stress and gives you a guide for the rites. Independent booking through Nusuk offers more flexibility and can cost less, but asks more planning of you. Both are valid; choose based on your confidence and budget.

How do I prepare my Ihram before flying?

Wash your Ihram once at home with an unscented detergent, dry it fully, and pack it clean and ready. Practise putting the garments on before you travel so you are comfortable at the Miqat.
