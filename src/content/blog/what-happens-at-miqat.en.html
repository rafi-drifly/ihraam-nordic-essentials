Miqat is the boundary at which a pilgrim must enter the state of Ihram before continuing toward Makkah. For most pilgrims travelling from Europe, this happens on the flight or at a designated point near Jeddah. The first time can feel uncertain, what to do, when to do it, and what to recite.

This is a practical step-by-step guide to what happens at Miqat, written for first-time pilgrims travelling from Sweden, the Nordics, and the EU.

What is Miqat?

Miqat is the geographic boundary established in Islamic tradition beyond which a pilgrim travelling toward Makkah must enter the state of Ihram. Crossing the Miqat without Ihram, when intending Hajj or Umrah, requires expiation (fidya).

There are five fixed Miqat locations, each serving pilgrims arriving from a different direction. Pilgrims flying from Europe typically cross the Miqat that corresponds to their flight path, most often Yalamlam (for those flying south of Makkah) or Dhul Hulayfah (for those routing through Madinah first).

Where are the Miqat boundaries?

Five boundaries serve pilgrims coming from different regions:

  • Dhul Hulayfah (Abyar Ali), for pilgrims coming from or through Madinah, the most commonly used Miqat for European pilgrims who visit Madinah first.
  • Al-Juhfah, for pilgrims coming from Syria, Egypt, and North Africa.
  • Qarn al-Manazil (As-Sail al-Kabir), for pilgrims coming from Najd and the Gulf region.
  • Yalamlam, for pilgrims coming from Yemen, much of South Asia, and many flights routing into Jeddah from the south.
  • Dhat Irq, for pilgrims coming from Iraq.

If you are unsure which Miqat applies to you, ask your Hajj or Umrah agency before departure. They will know the exact crossing point for your itinerary.

What do you do at Miqat?

The actions at Miqat are short, but the order matters. Here is the full step-by-step:

Step 1: Perform ghusl (or wudu)

Before reaching Miqat, complete a full ritual bath (ghusl) if circumstances allow. Wudu is acceptable if ghusl is not practical, for example, on a long flight.

Step 2: Change into Ihram

Remove all stitched clothing and wear the two unstitched pieces, the Izaar and the Ridaa. Use a belt to secure the Izaar. Wear sandals with no covered toes. Most pilgrims change in the airport changing room or, on flights, before the captain announces the approach to Miqat.

Step 3: Pray two rak'ahs

Where space and circumstances allow, pray two rak'ahs of nafl (voluntary prayer) before making the niyyah. This is sunnah, not obligatory.

Step 4: Make the niyyah (intention)

State your intention silently or aloud. The exact wording depends on whether you are performing Umrah, Hajj, or both. Examples: 'Labbayka Allahumma Umrah' (for Umrah), or 'Labbayka Allahumma Hajj' (for Hajj). Your Hajj guide or agency will advise on the specific niyyah for your type of pilgrimage (Tamattu, Ifrad, or Qiran).

Step 5: Begin reciting the Talbiyah

Once the niyyah is made, begin the Talbiyah: 'Labbayka Allahumma labbayk, labbayka la sharika laka labbayk, innal hamda wan-ni'mata laka wal-mulk, la sharika lak.' Continue reciting until you arrive at the Haram in Makkah, with breaks as needed.

What happens if you cross Miqat without Ihram?

Crossing Miqat without entering Ihram, when intending Hajj or Umrah, requires the pilgrim to return to the Miqat and enter Ihram there. If returning is not possible, expiation (fidya) is required, typically the slaughter of a sheep distributed to the poor in Makkah.

This is why entering Ihram before crossing Miqat is critical, and why agencies and airlines are usually clear about announcing the approach. If you are flying, change into Ihram before the announcement, not after.

Practical tips for first-time pilgrims at Miqat

Three small things make the experience smoother:

  • Wear your Ihram early. Many pilgrims change at the airport before boarding rather than rushing on the plane.
  • Have your Talbiyah and niyyah memorised or written down. Reading from a printed dua card avoids fumbling on the plane.
  • Keep your spare Ihram easily accessible in your carry-on. If anything happens to the set you're wearing, you don't want to wait for checked luggage in Jeddah.

Frequently asked questions

What is Miqat in Hajj?

Miqat is the geographic boundary at which pilgrims must enter the state of Ihram before continuing toward Makkah. There are five fixed Miqat points, each serving pilgrims arriving from a different region.

Do I have to put on Ihram exactly at Miqat?

Ihram must be entered before crossing Miqat. Most pilgrims put on Ihram earlier, at the airport before boarding, or on the plane before the announcement of the Miqat boundary.

Where is the Miqat for pilgrims flying from Europe?

It depends on your route. Pilgrims who visit Madinah first cross Dhul Hulayfah. Pilgrims flying directly to Jeddah on routes coming from the south often cross Yalamlam. Confirm with your Hajj agency before departure.

Can I delay entering Ihram until I land in Jeddah?

No. By the time you land, the plane has typically already crossed Miqat. Entering Ihram on the plane before the captain announces approach is the practical norm. Some airlines allow announcement notifications about ten minutes before crossing.

Do women change clothes at Miqat?

Female pilgrims do not wear the two-piece unstitched Ihram. They enter the state of Ihram in their normal modest clothing. The niyyah and Talbiyah are the same.

Conclusion

Miqat is the moment your Hajj truly begins. The actions are small (ghusl, change, niyyah, Talbiyah) but they mark the transition from traveller to pilgrim. Walk into it prepared, with your Ihram already washed, your spare in your carry-on, and your dua cards within reach.
