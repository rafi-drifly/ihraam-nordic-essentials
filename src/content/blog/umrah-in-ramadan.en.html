Umrah in Ramadan carries an immense reward: in a well-known hadith reported in Sahih al-Bukhari and Muslim, the Prophet (peace be upon him) taught that performing Umrah in Ramadan equals the reward of Hajj, in another wording, Hajj in his own company. That is why the two holy cities fill to capacity through the whole month, and especially the last ten nights.

This guide is for pilgrims travelling from Europe who want the reward without being overwhelmed by the crowds: what the reward means, how to time your rites, how to fast and stay strong while worshipping, and how to prepare your Ihram before you fly.

Why is Umrah in Ramadan so rewarded?

The central encouragement comes from an authentic hadith reported in both Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim, in which the Prophet (peace be upon him) told a woman that an Umrah performed in Ramadan is equal to the reward of Hajj, and in another narration, equal to Hajj performed in his company. Scholars are careful to explain what this means: the reward is multiplied and made equal in value, but performing Umrah in Ramadan does not remove the separate obligation of Hajj for the one who is able. A person who completes this Umrah has still not discharged their Hajj duty.

Understanding this keeps your intention clear. You travel to seek an enormous reward that Allah has attached to worship in this blessed month, not to substitute one pillar for another. Ramadan already multiplies good deeds, the Qur'an was revealed in it, and fasting, prayer and Umrah in the sacred precinct gather rare virtues in one journey.

The reality of the crowds

The reward is real, and so is the congestion. Ramadan is the single busiest season for Umrah. Makkah receives millions of pilgrims across the month, and the numbers climb sharply in the last ten nights as people arrive hoping to catch Laylat al-Qadr. The mataf (the open area around the Ka'bah where Tawaf is performed) can become extremely dense, and the walkways for Sa'i between Safa and Marwah move slowly at peak times.

Go in with honest expectations. There will be moments of waiting, of shuffling forward slowly, of praying far from the Ka'bah. None of that reduces your reward. The patience you show in a crowd, keeping your temper and protecting the people around you, is itself an act of worship that Ramadan multiplies.

Timing your Tawaf and Sa'i

Smart timing is the difference between a rushed, stressful rite and a calm one. A few patterns hold true through most of Ramadan:

  • Avoid the hour right after iftar and after Taraweeh. These are the heaviest windows, when everyone has broken their fast and comes to worship together.
  • The late-night and pre-dawn hours are often calmer for Tawaf and Sa'i, especially in the first two thirds of the month, though the last ten nights stay busy until suhoor.
  • Mid-morning, after Fajr and once the night crowd thins, can offer more space, though the sun and heat rise as the day goes on.
  • Watch the prayer times. Starting a Tawaf shortly before a congregational prayer means you may be paused mid-circuit, which is fine but adds time.

Tip: If the mataf on the ground floor is full, the upper levels and the roof are valid for Tawaf and are usually less crowded. They involve a longer walk per circuit, so pace yourself and carry water.

Fasting while performing the rites

Many pilgrims choose to perform their Umrah at night after iftar, when they have eaten and rehydrated, which is a sound and comfortable choice. Others perform it while fasting during the day. Both are valid. If you perform Umrah while fasting, be realistic about your energy: Tawaf and Sa'i together are a long walk, often more than three kilometres, on hard marble in a warm climate.

Hydration is the key issue, and it has to be managed in the narrow window between iftar and suhoor:

  • Drink steadily through the night rather than all at once. Zamzam is freely available throughout the Haram.
  • Include salts and fluids at suhoor, not only at iftar, so you start the day better hydrated.
  • Watch for the warning signs of heat and dehydration, dizziness, headache, cramping, and stop to rest if they appear.
  • If you are elderly, pregnant, managing a health condition, or simply exhausted, there is no shame in performing your rites at night or spreading your worship over several days.

Personal health circumstances and any question about fasting while travelling are matters to raise with a knowledgeable scholar or your group guide before you rely on a general rule.

Patience and safety in dense crowds

Crowd safety deserves real attention, particularly near the Ka'bah and at the entrances to the mataf. A few habits protect you and the people around you:

  • Never push toward the Black Stone or Maqam Ibrahim through a dense crowd. Kissing the Black Stone is a recommended act, not a requirement, and pointing to it from a distance while saying the takbir is fully accepted.
  • Keep children and elderly relatives close, and agree on a clear meeting point in case your group is separated.
  • Move with the flow, not against it. Sudden stops in a moving crowd are where injuries happen.
  • Carry minimal belongings, wear sandals you can manage in a throng, and keep your documents secure.

Follow the instructions of the Haram staff and security, who manage the flow of people every day and know the safe routes far better than any first-time pilgrim.

I'tikaf and the hope of Laylat al-Qadr

Many pilgrims plan their Ramadan Umrah around the last ten nights, hoping to witness Laylat al-Qadr, the night the Qur'an describes as better than a thousand months. Some also intend i'tikaf, a spiritual retreat in the mosque devoted to prayer, remembrance and Qur'an. If this is your hope, plan early: the last ten nights are the most crowded of the year, and accommodation near the Haram books out far in advance. Coordinate the practical side with your travel group or guide, and keep your worship consistent through the whole month rather than resting everything on a single night.

Preparing your Ihram before you fly

Ramadan Umrah is physically demanding, so the cloth you enter Ihram in matters more than usual. Two simple preparations make a real difference:

  • Pack a spare Ihram. After a long Tawaf and Sa'i in a warm, crowded Haram, your set will be damp with sweat. A clean, dry spare lets you enter each act of worship fresh, and it covers you if a piece is lost or torn.
  • Wash your Ihram at home before travelling. A first wash softens new fabric, removes any manufacturing residue, and makes the cloth more comfortable against the skin. Let it dry fully and fold it clean before packing.

Frequently asked questions

Does Umrah in Ramadan count as Hajj?

No. The authentic hadith reported in Sahih al-Bukhari and Muslim says Umrah in Ramadan equals the reward of Hajj, but scholars explain this refers to the reward, not to fulfilling the obligation. If you are able, you must still perform Hajj separately.

When is the best time to perform Tawaf during Ramadan?

The calmest windows are usually the late-night and pre-dawn hours, and mid-morning after the night crowd thins. Avoid the hour right after iftar and after Taraweeh, which are the busiest. The last ten nights stay crowded until suhoor.

Should I perform Umrah while fasting or after iftar?

Both are valid. Performing it at night after iftar is more comfortable because you are fed and rehydrated. If you perform it while fasting, pace yourself, rest when needed, and manage hydration carefully between iftar and suhoor.

How do I stay safe in the crowds near the Ka'bah?

Do not push toward the Black Stone; pointing to it with the takbir is accepted. Move with the flow of people, keep family close with an agreed meeting point, and follow the Haram staff, who manage the crowd every day.

How many Ihram sets should I bring for Ramadan Umrah?

At least two. A Ramadan Tawaf and Sa'i leave your set damp with sweat, so a clean, dry spare lets you worship fresh and covers you if a piece is lost or torn. Wash both at home before you travel.

Conclusion

Umrah in Ramadan offers a reward that few other journeys can match, alongside crowds that few other journeys can match. Approach it with a clear intention, a realistic plan for timing and hydration, patience in the crush, and an Ihram you have washed and prepared before you fly. Do that, and the season's difficulty becomes part of its reward.

May Allah accept your Umrah and grant you the blessings of this month.
